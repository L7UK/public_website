How to Pass a Cyber Essentials Plus Audit: 2026 Checklist

Joe Penetration Tester, Layer 7
cyber-essentials-pluscomplianceaudit

Most organisations that fail Cyber Essentials Plus fail for reasons they could have fixed in an afternoon: multi-factor authentication switched off on one cloud service, a laptop three patches behind, a piece of software the vendor stopped supporting last year. The audit is not designed to catch you out. It checks that the five controls you have already declared actually work on your live systems. Prepare for it properly and passing first time is the normal outcome.

This is the checklist we walk clients through before their formal audit. Layer 7 has been an IASME-licensed Cyber Essentials Certification Body since the scheme launched in 2014, and we run the hands-on Plus audit in-house, so this is what an assessor genuinely looks for, not a generic list.

First, the prerequisites

Two things have to be true before a Cyber Essentials Plus audit can go ahead:

  • You hold a current base Cyber Essentials certificate. Plus builds on it. If you are not sure which level you need, read Cyber Essentials vs Cyber Essentials Plus.
  • The Plus audit is booked within three months of that base certificate. Miss the window and the base assessment has to be redone. If you are starting from scratch, we do both together, which keeps you comfortably inside it.

What a Cyber Essentials Plus audit actually tests

Cyber Essentials Plus assesses the same five controls as the base level (firewalls, secure configuration, security update management, user access control and malware protection), but instead of taking your word for it, an assessor tests them on a representative sample of your live systems. In practice the audit covers:

  • An authenticated vulnerability scan of a sample of your in-scope devices, looking for missing patches and risky configuration.
  • A check that multi-factor authentication is enforced on every cloud service that supports it.
  • A malware protection test, including how your systems handle a known test file and malicious content arriving by email and web.
  • Patch verification: that critical and high-severity updates have been applied within the deadline, across your whole scope, not just the sampled devices.
  • A review of user accounts and administrative rights, including how admin access is separated from everyday use.

The assessor samples across each operating system and device type in your scope, so a mixed estate (Windows, macOS, mobile, servers) means a broader sample. Get the fundamentals consistent across the estate and the sample takes care of itself.

The pre-audit checklist, control by control

Work through this before you book the formal audit. If every row is green, the audit confirms what you already know.

In short: the audit checks the same five controls you self-declared for base Cyber Essentials, but proves them on live systems. If your inventory is honest, MFA is on everywhere, patching is inside 14 days across the whole estate, and unsupported software is gone, you have covered the reasons organisations actually fail.

The top reasons organisations fail, and how to avoid them

Almost every avoidable failure comes down to the same handful of issues. The 2026 rules (the “Danzell” question set on Requirements v3.3) made two of them automatic fails, so they matter more than ever.

Common Cyber Essentials Plus failures and how to fix them before the audit
Reason for failureWhy it fails the auditFix before you book
MFA not enabled everywhereSince April 2026, a cloud service that supports MFA but has it switched off is an automatic failTurn on MFA for every user on every cloud service, using an NCSC-approved method; app or hardware tokens beat SMS
Missed patch deadlineCritical or high-severity updates left beyond 14 days fail, and now the whole scope is checked, not just the sampleApply patch management uniformly across every in-scope device; do not treat the audit sample as the finish line
Unsupported software still in useSoftware the vendor no longer patches cannot be secured, so it failsRemove, replace or fully segregate anything out of support before the audit
Cloud services left out of scopeThe 2026 formal definition of “cloud service” means SaaS, IaaS and PaaS can no longer be excludedInventory every cloud platform, including shadow IT, and bring it into scope
Admin accounts used for daily workEveryday browsing and email from an admin account breaches access controlGive admins a separate standard account for routine work; keep admin rights for admin tasks

The key takeaway: MFA gaps and late patching are the two failures the 2026 rules turned into automatic fails, and both are fixable before you ever book the audit. A readiness review catches them cheaply.

What to have ready on the day

A smooth audit day comes down to access and paperwork being in place before the assessor starts:

  • A named technical contact who can make changes and answer questions in real time.
  • Remote access to the sampled devices, or the devices physically available if the audit is on-site.
  • Your asset inventory and list of in-scope cloud services.
  • Evidence that patches are current and MFA is enforced (screens, reports or a walkthrough).
  • Admin credentials ready for the authenticated scan, handled securely.

Most Cyber Essentials Plus audits now run fully remotely, which is usually the faster route. We agree the approach with you at scoping, so there are no surprises.

The single best thing you can do: a readiness review

The organisations that pass first time almost all do one thing beforehand: they have someone check the controls against the current requirements before the formal audit, while findings are still cheap to fix. That is the entire point of a readiness review. We run one, tell you exactly where you stand, and help you close any gaps, so the formal audit confirms a pass rather than uncovering surprises. From an agreed scope, we can often complete the audit itself in a single day.

Cyber Essentials Plus audit FAQs

How do I prepare for a Cyber Essentials Plus audit? Make sure your asset inventory is honest, multi-factor authentication is enabled on every cloud service, critical patches are applied within 14 days across your whole scope, unsupported software is removed, and admin accounts are separated from everyday use. A readiness review before the formal audit catches anything left over.

What does the Cyber Essentials Plus audit test? An assessor tests the five controls on a sample of your live systems: an authenticated vulnerability scan, a check that MFA is enforced on cloud services, a malware protection test, verification that critical updates are applied in time across the whole scope, and a review of user and admin accounts.

Why do organisations fail Cyber Essentials Plus? Most failures come down to MFA not being enabled everywhere, critical patches left beyond 14 days, unsupported software still in use, cloud services left out of scope, or admin accounts used for daily work. All are fixable before the audit.

How long does a Cyber Essentials Plus audit take? From an agreed scope, we can often complete the audit in a single day. A larger or more complex estate takes longer, which we confirm at scoping.

Can I do the audit remotely? Yes. Most Cyber Essentials Plus audits now run fully remotely, which is usually the faster route. On-site makes sense when devices cannot be accessed remotely or you prefer an assessor in the room.

Do I need base Cyber Essentials before the Plus audit? Yes. You need a current base Cyber Essentials certificate, and the Plus audit must be completed within three months of it. We can do both together.
